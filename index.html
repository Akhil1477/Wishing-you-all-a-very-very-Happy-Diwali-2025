The History of Diwali

Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is one of the most ancient and significant festivals in Indian culture, celebrated for over 2,500 years.

The festival commemorates the return of Lord Rama to Ayodhya after 14 years of exile and his victory over the demon king Ravana. The people of Ayodhya lit oil lamps to welcome their beloved prince home.

Diwali symbolizes the spiritual victory of light over darkness, good over evil, and knowledge over ignorance. It's a time for new beginnings, forgiveness, and the celebration of life itself.

Diwali Traditions

Lighting Diyas

Oil lamps are lit in and around homes to invite prosperity and drive away darkness and evil spirits.

Rangoli Designs

Colorful patterns are created at entrances using colored powders, flowers, and rice to welcome guests and deities.

Fireworks

The sky is illuminated with spectacular fireworks displays, symbolizing the joy and celebration of the festival.

Sweets & Feasts

Families prepare and share traditional sweets and delicacies, spreading happiness and sweetness in relationships.

New Clothes

Wearing new clothes symbolizes a fresh start and the spirit of renewal that Diwali brings.

Lakshmi Puja

Prayers are offered to Goddess Lakshmi for wealth, prosperity, and well-being throughout the coming year.
